A Reflection on McLuhan, M. ‘The Medium is the Message'

Today I read a paper called The Medium is the Message by Marshall McLuhan. Though the piece is representative of its time in a few ways, such as treatment of Africans as universally illiterate, it is also foresightful in its conception of “electric media” as a force of significant societal change, and thoughtful in considering the connection inherent between the medium of transmission and the message within. After reading, I was aware of a significant prediction in the piece. McLuhan mentions that “each of the media is also a powerful weapon with which to clobber other media and other groups. The result is that the present age has been one of multiple civil wars that are not limited to the world of art and entertainment” (McLuhan, 1964).

This has proven true in every new form of media we have created since McLuhan wrote this paper, perhaps even progressively so, with the newest forms of media, social media, featuring extraordinary amounts of direct conflict (Anderson, 2018) (Leighton, 2019). It is also the case that this trend of increasing conflict follows the trend of increasing interactability of new media. If this understanding is correct, then over time our conflicts in the real world may continue to be superseded by conflicts in the progressively interactable realm of digital media. It may be the case then, that as these new media become the “technological staples”, the “unique cultural flavor” (McLuhan, 1964) they bring may be one of division and discord.

The Memex and the Message

Vannevar Bush’s paper As We May Think was written half a century before his ideas manifested in the World Wide Web. On its 65th anniversary, the paper was reviewed by Simon Harper in ‘As We May Think’ At 65, discussing the value and predictive power it holds even today. Noted in Harper’s paper, is Bush’s intention for the Memex to be a mechanical device, elucidating the fact that all technologies Bush discussed take a significantly different physical form today than he predicted. Nonetheless, ideas and insights from his paper remain deeply relevant, such as the idea that the Memex would be a “device for individual use”, adapted to its user and their specific needs. Harper makes this idea explicit, saying “The key point here is the idea that human understanding and cognition can be enhanced by tailoring information, and the associations between this information, to the way that the human brain is organised”.

Online services such as Spotify, Netflix, and Facebook have taken this concept and applied it to human experience, enhancing each user’s time on their platform by providing content tailored to the individual. In the case of Netflix, different users are shown different cover art for the same shows and movies depending on a digital psychological profile created by algorithmic processing of their viewing habits, with the purpose of providing quality information about each show or movie to users who process information uniquely. The contrast of Bush’s spot on “vision of how information could be manageable even on a large scale by association based on the experience of the individual” and his far less accurate idea of the medium through which that information would pass calls into question the strength of the idea that the medium is the message.

I have discussed Marshall McLuhan’s idea of the message being inseparable from the message on this blog before. McLuhan provides historical evidence to back up his theory, such as the effect of print media on the French Revolution. Yet, looking back from McLuhan’s perspective, and forward from Bush’s, it seems plausible that the medium does not so much affect the message as it enables new kinds of messages to be popularized. Bush predicted the individualisation of the message not because his preferred medium of “rods, gears, pulleys and rotors” imbues information with an essence of the individual, but because computation allows fundamentally new kinds of messages to be transmitted. Perhaps print only provided the ideas of liberté, égalité, and fraternité a mode of transmission, and had radio instead of print emerged as the technology of transmission at the time, a very similar French Revolution would have occurred, with the same message of liberté, égalité, and fraternité as its engine.

What Do Users Have To Say About Design?

The paper Revealing the Hidden Rationality of User Browsing Behaviour is another older paper I have read recently. It contains a data-driven analysis of three different types of communicative techniques within web design; Visual, Verbal, and Neutral. It asks the question of whether different users have preferences for one type of design over another, with the hope that the answer could provide insights into how personalisation might be used to enhance educational web applications. With the advent of significant remote learning activities in early 2020, and with their continued presence up until the present day, even a small design change on educational websites could have a large impact.


Before analysing the paper’s finding, it is worth examining when and how it arrived at those answers. The first issue worth highlighting is the date the paper was published, back in 2007. For average academic writing, this is not terribly old, but technology moves faster than other fields, and web technology specifically so. As such, the findings may not be as relevant today as they were then. Secondly, the sample of users tested, only 144, was made up entirely of Computer Science students from the University of Nottingham. This sample is not a representative slice of any population, as women have historically been underrepresented of in Computer Science, and older users experience the web, and technology in general, differently than those of average college age. Additionally, half of the sessions did not switch between the modes, leaving the sample size for some calculations at only 84.


Now that some important shortcomings of the paper have been addressed, the findings can be better understood in context. Likely as expected, the more visual modes faired significantly better than the Verbal mode. This reinforces the idea the visual stimulus such as images is important for maintaining the attention of a user, and that text-heavy design is not as accessible. The paper found little evidence of mode switching as a preference driven activity, meaning there it provides no evidence personalization would improve learning outcomes. However, the paper did find that mode switching could be used to alleviate boredom. This finding has the implication that monotonous design may lead to less attention from users and poorer educational outcomes, and design which introduces a new look to an application after some time may improve attention and lead to better educational outcomes. This is not a surprising outcome, but it is clear in the data and easily actionable by web designers. Further study is needed to reproduce this outcome more robustly, but in the meantime, designing applications with more interesting and varied interfaces may not be a bad habit.


References

Brown, E., Brailsford, T., Fisher, T. and van der Eijk, C., 2007, September. Revealing the hidden rationality of user browsing behaviour. In Proceedings of the eighteenth conference on Hypertext and hypermedia (pp. 85-94).



Internet Inequities


Towards a study of information geographies:(im)mutable augmentations and a mapping of the geographies of information is a paper which, through vast amounts of data superbly visualized, demonstrates the inequities that exist on the Internet. Though this study is from before oceanic currents worsened the African continent’s access to the Internet, its relevance is not diminished but rather amplified, as the inequities it outlines have been amplified. Of particular interest to me, and I believe to South Africa, is the discrepancy between the population of internet users and domain names, Wikipedia edits, and GitHub commits outlined in the study. This represents a difference in agency online, and a difference in who is speaking and who is listening.


The Internet is powerful. This is not a disputed fact. Having a voice on the Internet is having power, and this power can be used to spread truth or lies, and mobilize hate or compassion. When oppressors are the ones with a voice, they use it for their own gain at the expense of others, and are able to spread lies and hate on a whim. At no time was this clearer than during Donald Trump’s four years as president of the United States, with his prolific use of Twitter inspiring multiple acts of violence. But when the oppressed can speak up and be heard, truth can break through and instigate change. At no time was this clearer than during the Black Lives Matter protests in 2020, ignited in response to a video of George Floyd’s murder at the hands of four police officers. The video was widely shared on social media, spoken so loudly and so ubiquitously that it could not go unheard, even by those who tried to ignore it. The changes brought about because of that voice, amplified by millions of black people with lived experience of racial discrimination around the world, though far from enough, is significant and important. Lives are demonstrably better today than they were in 2020 because of that voice.


But South Africans and other Africans today do not have a voice like those in Europe or North America, and we go unheard because of it. We do not even write our own stories. They are written by others. This is exemplified by the data on Wikipedia edits for the African continent, where only 5% of edits on articles about Africa are made by Africans. This kind of digital colonialism extends to South Africa as well, where we write less than half of Wikipedia articles about ourselves. Structural changes are needed to address this issue, such as Wikipedia servers located in Africa for when undersea fibre is disrupted. I hope my own small actions, such as the GitHub commit that will send this article online, push the world slightly in the right direction.


References

Graham, M., De Sabbata, S. and Zook, M.A., 2015. Towards a study of information geographies:(im) mutable augmentations and a mapping of the geographies of information. Geo: Geography and environment, 2(1), pp.88-105.
